From v1 to v3: Solving a supply-side platform problem

How I evolved a content delivery platform from manual dumps to AI-generated content, growing it to 500K+ users across 10+ states.

Product Weekly Practice
Role Product Owner (v1→v3)
Impact 500K+ students, 10+ states

The Problem

Weekly Practice is a chatbot that delivers curriculum-aligned quizzes and homework to students. In v1, it relied entirely on state governments manually providing question banks — a slow, error-prone process that blocked expansion.

Every new state meant months of waiting for content. Completion rates were declining because the experience felt static and one-size-fits-all.

What I Did

V1 → V2: I identified declining completion rates and diagnosed two root causes — no teacher involvement and no adaptability to student performance. I designed two features:

  • Homework by My Teacher: Teachers assign weekly homework through a companion chatbot, connecting classroom teaching to practice.
  • Advanced Practice: Students scoring below a configurable cutoff get additional practice on the same learning outcomes.

I also built priority configuration — states could decide whether students see the quiz or homework first, making the product adaptable to different pedagogical priorities.

V2 → V3 (currently building): Even with v2's improvements, scaling content remained the bottleneck. States refused to host their question banks on our servers due to trust and policy concerns.

Instead of pushing harder on the same approach, I led the shift to AI-generated assessment content — removing the supply-side dependency entirely.

Key Outcomes

  • Grew from initial deployment to 500K+ students across 10+ states
  • V2's configurability unlocked new state onboarding that wasn't possible with v1's rigid model
  • V3 (in progress) eliminates the content dependency that had blocked expansion for months

Key Product Decisions

Chose teacher agency over better quiz algorithms — bet that involvement drives adoption more than optimization. It worked.

  • Made priority settings state-configurable rather than hardcoding a default — more engineering effort upfront but allowed us to serve states with opposing pedagogical philosophies.
  • V3's AI shift wasn't about "adding AI" — it was the only way to remove a structural dependency. AI was the right tool for this specific constraint.

When your biggest client refuses to use your product

How I retained Rajasthan as a client by building an SDK integration instead of forcing user migration — and fixed a data integrity crisis along the way.

Product School Based Assessment
Role Product Manager
Impact 1M+ assessments, 10+ states

The Problem

Our School Based Assessment (SBA) platform had captured 1M+ assessments across 10+ states. But Rajasthan — one of our largest and most important deployments — refused to move their teachers to our platform.

They had an existing app (Shikshak App) that teachers used daily, and the state education department didn't want the overhead of training hundreds of thousands of teachers on a new tool.

Separately, I discovered that manual registry dumps had created duplicate student records across schools — the same student appearing in two different schools' data, corrupting assessment results.

What I Did

Problem 1 — Rajasthan integration:

I proposed a different approach: instead of forcing migration, build an SDK to embed our assessment webview inside Rajasthan's existing Shikshak App. Teachers would never leave their familiar interface. Our assessment engine would run inside it.

I wrote the integration specs, coordinated with engineering on the SDK architecture, and managed testing with Rajasthan's technical team. The approach let us retain the client with zero user disruption.

Problem 2 — Data integrity:

I built a webview to simplify the assessment process for teachers (replacing the complex chatbot flow with a cleaner UI for actual assessment capture). I also discovered the duplicate records issue, traced it to the manual registry upload process, and architected validation logic that identified and cleaned duplicate entries across the dataset.

Key Outcomes

  • Retained Rajasthan as a client — zero teacher migration required
  • SDK approach became a template for other states with similar resistance
  • Assessment webview simplified the teacher experience
  • Validation logic cleaned the registry data, ensuring accurate assessment records
  • SBA platform now at 1M+ assessments across 10+ states

Key Product Decisions

Chose integration over migration — met the client where they were instead of where we wanted them to be.

  • Built the webview because the chatbot interface was too complex for multi-step assessment flows — not every problem is best solved in a chat UI.
  • The SDK approach has reuse potential — any state that refuses to adopt our app can get the same integration.

Building a field inspection product from zero

Competitive research across 7+ state government systems revealed a clear market gap — then I wrote the PRD, designed offline-first flows, and shipped in 2 states.

Product School Inspection App
Role Product Manager (0→1)
Shipped Madhya Pradesh & Sikkim

The Problem

School inspections across Indian states were paper-based. Inspecting officers would visit schools, fill forms manually, and submit reports that went into filing cabinets.

There was no digital tracking, no accountability loop (inspectors couldn't verify if their recommendations were implemented), and no way to follow up systematically.

Research

I studied 7+ existing state government inspection systems:

  • Rajasthan: ShalaDarpan + ShalaSamblan
  • Gujarat: SSA CRC Monitoring App
  • West Bengal: m-PARIDARSHAN
  • And 4+ others across different states

Key finding: No existing solution combined intelligent scheduling + pre-filled dynamic forms + spot assessments + closed-loop ticketing in a single, state-scalable system. Every solution was state-locked and lacked an accountability layer.

Product Design

I wrote the PRD covering:

  • Offline-first inspection: Complete inspections without internet, auto-sync when reconnected
  • Task-based assessment: Mark items Yes/No with mandatory comments on negative findings
  • Photo/video evidence capture
  • Closed-loop feedback: Head of School receives tasks, updates status, Inspector validates on revisit
  • Session management: 24-hour save window for incomplete inspections

Key Outcomes

  • Shipped v1 in Madhya Pradesh and Sikkim
  • Currently building v2 with geo-tagging and push notifications
  • The competitive research identified a clear gap that no state system had addressed

Key Product Decisions

Offline-first was non-negotiable — inspectors visit rural schools with unreliable connectivity.

  • Made comments mandatory on "No" responses — forces inspectors to explain, not just check boxes.
  • Designed conflict resolution via timestamp-based last-write-wins for concurrent data syncs.
  • The closed-loop (Inspector → HoS → Inspector reinspection) was the core differentiator — every other system was one-directional.
